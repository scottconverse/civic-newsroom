# Filing Public Records Requests

**When online sources fail, public records requests are your fallback. Every state has a law giving you the right to access government records. This guide covers the basics.**

---

## The Basics

Every U.S. state has an open records law (sometimes called sunshine law, freedom of information law, or public records act). At the federal level, it's FOIA (Freedom of Information Act). At the state and local level, each state has its own version with its own name, deadlines, and exemptions.

You do not need to be a journalist to file a records request. Any person can request public records from their city, county, or state government.

---

| State | Law Name | Common Abbreviation |
|-------|----------|-------------------|
| California | California Public Records Act | CPRA |
| Colorado | Colorado Open Records Act | CORA |
| Florida | Florida Public Records Act | Chapter 119 |
| Illinois | Freedom of Information Act | IL FOIA |
| New York | Freedom of Information Law | FOIL |
| Ohio | Ohio Public Records Act | ORC 149.43 |
| Texas | Texas Public Information Act | TPIA |

To find your state's law: search `[your state] public records law` or `[your state] open records act`.

## What You Can Request

Almost any document created or received by a government agency in the course of its business:

- Meeting minutes, agendas, and packets
- Emails between city officials about official business
- Contracts and agreements
- Budget documents and financial records
- Building permits and inspection reports
- Code violation notices
- Police reports (with some exemptions)
- Personnel records (limited — usually just salary and title)
- Consultant reports and studies
- Public comment submissions
- Correspondence with developers, applicants, or contractors

---

## What's Usually Exempt

Most states exempt certain categories:

- Active law enforcement investigations
- Attorney-client privileged communications
- Personnel evaluations and medical records
- Trade secrets submitted by private companies
- Certain security-related documents
- Records where release would endanger someone's safety
- Deliberative process (draft documents, internal deliberations — varies by state)

If an agency denies your request, they must cite the specific exemption. You can appeal.

## How to File a Request

### Step 1: Find the Right Contact
- Search: `[your city name] public records request`
- Look for: City Clerk, Records Division, or a dedicated online portal
- Many cities now have online request forms

### Step 2: Write the Request

Keep it specific. Vague requests get slow responses or denials.

**Good request:**
> "I am requesting all building permits issued for properties at 123 Main Street between January 1, 2025 and March 1, 2026, including application materials, inspection reports, and any code violation notices."

**Bad request:**
> "I want all documents about development in downtown."

### Step 3: Submit

- Use the city's online portal if one exists
- Otherwise, email the City Clerk or Records Division
- Some jurisdictions still accept paper requests (but email creates a paper trail)

### Step 4: Wait

Response times vary by state:
- Some states require response within 3 business days (Florida is notably fast)
- Many states allow 7-10 business days
- Complex requests may take longer — the agency should notify you of the timeline

### Step 5: Review What You Get

Check for:
- Redactions — Are they appropriate? Each redaction should cite a specific exemption.
- Completeness — Did you get everything you asked for?
- Format — If you asked for electronic records, did they give you paper? (Most states require electronic delivery if records exist electronically)

## Sample Request Templates

### Building Permits
> Pursuant to [YOUR STATE'S OPEN RECORDS LAW], I request copies of all building permit applications, issued permits, and inspection reports for [ADDRESS] from [START DATE] to [END DATE].

### City Council Communications
> Pursuant to [YOUR STATE'S OPEN RECORDS LAW], I request copies of all email communications between [CITY OFFICIAL/DEPARTMENT] and [PARTY] regarding [TOPIC] from [START DATE] to [END DATE].

### Contracts
> Pursuant to [YOUR STATE'S OPEN RECORDS LAW], I request a copy of the contract between [CITY NAME] and [VENDOR/CONTRACTOR] for [PROJECT/SERVICE], including all amendments and change orders.

### Budget Documents
> Pursuant to [YOUR STATE'S OPEN RECORDS LAW], I request copies of all budget transfer documents, fund reallocation memos, and reserve draw-down authorizations for the [DEPARTMENT] from [START DATE] to [END DATE].

## Costs

Most jurisdictions charge for records:
- Many provide the first 25-100 pages free
- After that, copying fees typically range from $0.10-$0.25 per page
- Electronic records are often free or very cheap
- Some states waive fees for requests in the public interest (journalists and civic organizations can often get fee waivers)

If a cost estimate seems unreasonably high, narrow your request or ask if electronic delivery would reduce the cost.

---

## If Your Request Is Denied

1. Ask for the specific legal exemption cited
2. Consider narrowing your request to exclude exempt categories
3. File an appeal (most states have an appeals process — often to the attorney general or a records ombudsman)
4. Contact your state's press association or freedom of information organization — many provide free guidance

## Tips

**Be polite but firm.** Records custodians are people doing their jobs. A respectful request gets faster results than a hostile one. But don't accept vague denials — insist on specific legal citations.

**Start narrow, then expand.** A focused request for specific documents gets results faster than a broad request for "everything about X."

**Keep a log.** Document when you submitted, who you contacted, what response you received, and any deadlines. This is useful if you need to appeal.

**Electronic is better.** Always request electronic copies. They're cheaper, faster, and easier to search.

**The request itself is a signal.** Sometimes just filing a records request prompts the city to proactively release information. And the fact that you're asking tells them someone is paying attention.
