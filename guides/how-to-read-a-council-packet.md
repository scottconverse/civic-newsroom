# How to Read a Council Packet

**City council packets are the richest single source of civic intelligence in any municipality. They're also intimidating if you've never read one. This guide breaks down what's in them and what to look for.**

---

## What Is a Council Packet?

A council packet (also called an agenda packet, board packet, or meeting packet) is the bundle of documents published before a city council meeting. It contains everything council members will review, discuss, and vote on.

Packets typically run 50-300+ pages. They're usually posted as PDFs on the city's agenda portal 3-7 days before the meeting.

---

## The Anatomy of a Packet

Most packets follow a standard structure:

### 1. The Agenda (1-3 pages)
The table of contents. Lists every item the council will address, in order. This tells you what's happening at a glance.

**Look for:** Items marked "Public Hearing" (residents can speak), "Ordinance" (new law), "Resolution" (formal decision), or "Contract" (money being spent).

### 2. Consent Agenda Items
A batch of items the council plans to approve as a group, without individual discussion. These are supposed to be routine and non-controversial.

**Why this matters:** Items buried in the consent agenda sometimes aren't routine at all. Budget transfers, contract renewals, and policy changes can be slipped in here. Any council member can "pull" an item from consent for separate discussion, but they rarely do. This is where the Black Desk's "Procedural Pivot" detection is most useful.

### 3. Staff Reports
For each significant agenda item, city staff write a report explaining what they're recommending and why. This is the most information-rich part of the packet.

**What to look for in a staff report:**
- **The recommendation** — What does staff want council to do?
- **The financial impact** — How much does it cost? Where does the money come from?
- **The alternatives** — What other options were considered? (Sometimes listed, sometimes not)
- **The timeline** — When does this take effect?
- **Citations** — What documents, studies, or consultants are referenced? (These are source discovery gold)
- **Attachments** — Maps, contracts, engineering studies, public comments

### 4. Ordinances and Resolutions
The actual legal text being voted on. Ordinances are local laws; resolutions are formal statements or decisions.

**What to look for:**
- What specifically is changing? (Look for redlined text showing additions/deletions)
- When does it take effect?
- Who does it affect?
- Is this a first reading or second reading? (Most cities require two readings before an ordinance becomes law — the real debate usually happens at first reading)

### 5. Public Hearing Materials
For items requiring a public hearing (usually development projects, zoning changes, or budget decisions), the packet may include:
- Application materials
- Maps and site plans
- Letters from residents (for and against)
- Staff analysis of the proposal

### 6. Contracts and Agreements
When the city is spending money or entering agreements. The packet usually includes the actual contract or a summary.

**What to look for:**
- Dollar amount and duration
- Vendor/contractor name (search them — who are they?)
- Scope of work
- Whether this is a new contract or a renewal

## What Makes a Story

Not everything in a packet is newsworthy. Here's what to prioritize:

**Money being spent:** Any appropriation over $50,000 deserves attention. Who's getting paid? For what? Was it competitively bid?

**Zoning and development:** New building projects, rezoning requests, and variance applications directly affect neighborhoods. Look for the address, the developer, the size, and whether neighbors have commented.

**Policy changes:** New ordinances or amendments to existing ones change the rules residents live under. What's changing? Who does it affect? Who asked for the change?

**Appointments:** Who's being appointed to boards and commissions? These are the people who make planning, zoning, parks, and utility decisions.

**Items pulled from consent:** If a council member pulls something from the consent agenda, it's because they think it deserves scrutiny. Pay attention.

**Items tabled or withdrawn:** An item removed from the agenda without explanation is a signal worth tracking. Why was it pulled? The Black Desk's "Missing Document" detection focuses on exactly this.

---

## Practical Tips

**Read the agenda first.** Skim the 1-3 page agenda to identify which items are worth reading the full staff report for. You don't need to read every page of a 200-page packet.

**Ctrl+F is your friend.** Search the PDF for dollar amounts, specific addresses, company names, or keywords related to issues you're tracking.

**Follow the citations.** Staff reports reference consultants, studies, state agencies, and other data sources. These are leads for your Sources Registry.

**Check the attachments.** Letters from residents, maps, and contracts are often more revealing than the staff summary.

**Compare to the previous meeting's minutes.** What was discussed last time? Did the council direct staff to come back with something? Did they? If not, that's a signal.

---

## Where to Find Packets

- Your city's agenda management portal (PrimeGov, Granicus, Legistar, etc.)
- City Clerk's page on the city website
- Sometimes linked from the city calendar

If packets aren't posted online, call the City Clerk and ask. Under most states' open meetings laws, agenda materials must be available to the public.
