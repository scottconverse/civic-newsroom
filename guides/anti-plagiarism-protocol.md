# Anti-Plagiarism Protocol

**This guide documents the lessons learned about the line between aggregation and original reporting when using AI for civic transparency work. These rules exist because we crossed that line once and got caught.**

---

## The Problem

When you ask an AI to research a topic and write a story, it will naturally synthesize everything it finds — including other journalists' reporting. The result can look like original work but is actually a rewrite of someone else's journalism. This is plagiarism, even if unintentional.

This problem is structural, not moral. The AI isn't trying to steal. It's doing what it does: blending available information into a coherent narrative. But the result is the same — and it can trigger legitimate legal and ethical complaints.

---

## The Core Rule

**Other journalists' work can tell you WHAT TO LOOK FOR. It cannot be your SOURCE.**

If the local paper reports that council discussed building heights, that tells you to search for the council agenda, the meeting minutes, or the official city bulletin about building heights. You then report from those primary documents.

What you cannot do is read the paper's story, reword it, and publish it as your own civic reporting.

---

## The Three Tests

Before publishing any story, apply these tests:

### Test 1: The Primary Source Test
Can you trace every factual claim in your story to a specific primary document? (Agenda, minutes, permit, budget, official announcement, public record)

If not — the claim either needs a primary source or needs to be removed.

### Test 2: The Subtraction Test
If you removed all information that originally came from other news outlets, would this story still exist?

If not — you're rewriting their work, not doing original reporting.

### Test 3: The Mirror Test
Does the structure of your story resemble the structure of any news article you (or the AI) read while researching?

If yes — restructure from scratch using primary sources as your organizational framework.

---

## What TO Do

**Use journalism as a lead generator:**
- "The paper reports council discussed X" → Search for the city agenda item about X
- "The TV station covered a new development at [address]" → Search the permit database for that address
- "A reporter tweeted about a budget shortfall" → Search the city's quarterly financial report

**Ground everything in primary documents:**
- City council agendas, packets, and minutes
- Official city press releases and announcements
- Official municipal YouTube channel transcripts (auto-generated transcripts from the city's official channel are Tier A — they're machine transcriptions of government proceedings)
- Meeting recordings and audio/video
- Budget documents and financial reports
- Permit applications and zoning filings
- State regulatory filings
- Court records

**When you can't find a primary source:**
- Publish a headline-only note: "[Paper] reports [topic]. Unable to confirm via primary sources."
- OR skip the story entirely
- OR file a public records request and hold the story until you have primary documentation

---

## What NOT to Do

**Do not paraphrase another journalist's sentences.** Even reworded, the structure and framing belong to them.

**Do not follow another outlet's article organization.** If they led with the human interest angle and built to the policy implications, don't follow that structure. Start from your primary documents and let your story's structure emerge from what's in the record.

**Do not adopt another outlet's framing.** If the paper frames a development as "controversial," that's their editorial judgment. Your story should present the facts from primary sources and let readers form their own conclusions.

**Do not present journalism-sourced facts as if you discovered them.** If the only reason you know about a topic is because another outlet covered it, be transparent about that.

---

## The Exception: When Journalism IS the Story

Sometimes the journalism itself is newsworthy:
- A newspaper publishes an investigation revealing new facts
- A media outlet is involved in a legal dispute about access
- A reporter's coverage becomes a public controversy
- A news organization closes or significantly reduces coverage

In these cases, cite the journalism explicitly: "The [Paper] reported on [date] that [finding], based on their investigation." This is attribution, not plagiarism.

---

## Attribution Examples

**BAD (plagiarism risk):**
> Council members discussed building heights during study sessions, emphasizing that code changes would require public engagement.

This sounds like you attended the meeting. But if you learned it from the paper, this is rewriting their reporting.

**GOOD (proper attribution when primary source unavailable):**
> HEADLINE-ONLY NOTE: [Paper name] reports council height discussion. City's official meeting bulletin does not reference this topic. Cannot confirm via primary sources.

**BETTER (primary source confirmed):**
> City's February 3 study session agenda lists "Discussion on building height amendments" according to the official city calendar. The agenda item references a staff memorandum on height regulations in the downtown overlay district.

---

## Journalism Credit (Mandatory)

When a Tier B outlet's reporting led you to a story that you then independently verified from Tier A primary sources, you must credit the originating outlet. This is not optional.

**Format:** Place one of these in the closing paragraph of your story:
- "[Paper name] first reported on [topic]."
- "[Paper name] coverage provides additional context."

**What Journalism Credit is NOT:**
- It is not a source citation for any specific factual claim (your claims must still be grounded in Tier A sources)
- It does not replace the requirement to independently verify from primary documents
- It does not allow you to use the outlet's reporting as your source

**Why it matters:** Journalism Credit is a professional courtesy that acknowledges the broader civic information ecosystem. When your independent analysis of public records overlaps with topics that professional journalists have already covered, crediting their prior work is both ethical and practical. It builds trust with local media, demonstrates transparency about how topics were identified, and reinforces that this system exists to expand civic coverage, not duplicate existing reporting.

---

## Why This Matters

Local newspapers are struggling. Many have closed. The ones that survive operate on thin margins with small staffs. When an AI system rewrites their reporting and publishes it for free, it undermines the economic foundation of the journalism that identified the story in the first place.

Civic Newsroom exists to fill gaps in civic coverage, not to replace the journalists who are still working. The anti-plagiarism protocol ensures that this system adds to the local information ecosystem rather than extracting from it.

If the local paper covers something and you can't add original reporting from primary sources, the right thing to do is nothing. Let their story stand. Focus your effort on the stories nobody else is covering — the ones buried in council packets, permit databases, and budget documents that no reporter has time to read.

That's where the real value of this system lives.
