There's a Free AI Civic Transparency Toolkit for Your City. Here's How It Works.

In 2005, there were roughly 9,000 newspapers in the U.S. Today, fewer than 6,000. More than 2,500 communities have lost their local paper entirely.

City councils still meet. Budgets still get passed. Contracts still get awarded. But nobody is reading any of it.

Longmont, Colorado is one of those places. Population 105,000. The city publishes 200-page council packets before every meeting. The information is technically public. Almost nobody reads it.

That problem led to the Civic Newsroom — a nine-agent AI pipeline that turns public records into accessible civic reports. And as of today, it's open source and free.

—

It's not a single prompt. It's a complete editorial system: an aggregator producing 15–25 leads daily, a story expansion engine, a speculative signal radar that finds what's missing, an adversarial verification desk, an integrity checker, an anti-plagiarism protocol, First Amendment counsel, and a plain-language translator.

The system was tested against live municipal data. It produced real stories. More importantly, testing revealed exactly how AI-assisted civic reporting fails — and every lesson got built into the architecture.

—

Why the safeguards matter:

AI hallucinates. So the system includes a mandatory four-gate verification protocol that can't be bypassed.

AI produces one-sided analysis. So an adversarial protocol forces counter-narrative searching before any contested claim moves toward publication.

AI inadvertently plagiarizes. So the Civic Grounding Protocol ensures every claim traces to primary public records — not other journalists' work. When another outlet's reporting led to the story, a mandatory Journalism Credit acknowledges their contribution.

—

Every community with a city council and a public records portal can use these tools. A system to make those records accessible doesn't need to be proprietary. It needs to be available to anyone who wants to use it.

That's why the entire toolkit is open source. Every prompt. Every protocol. Every template. Every guide.

The repo includes nine generalized prompts, six blank templates, a pipeline cheat sheet, guides for people who've never done this, and a working Longmont example.

No journalism background required. No coding. Just a free AI chatbot, your city's agenda portal and YouTube channel URLs, and the willingness to understand what local government is actually doing.

Try it in your town. Set it up, contribute your config back. Improve the prompts, share them. Publish stories, tell us about it.

The information to hold local government accountable already exists in public records. What's been missing is someone reading them. Now there's a system for that.

And it's yours.
